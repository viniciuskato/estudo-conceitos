What this compendium covers

Cardiac anatomy describes the macroscopic and microscopic structure of the heart — its four chambers, four valves, dedicated blood supply system (coronary circulation), electrical conduction system, fibroserious envelope (pericardium), and topographic relationship with neighboring structures in the mediastinum. It is the foundation without which no cardiac pathology can be understood mechanistically: aortic valve stenosis, anterior wall myocardial infarction, atrial fibrillation, and pericardial tamponade only make anatomofunctional sense when grounded in structure.

The heart is a hollow muscular organ located in the middle mediastinum, between the two lungs, above the diaphragm, and behind the sternum. It weighs between 250 and 350 g in adults (varying with sex, height, and physical conditioning) and pumps approximately 5 liters of blood per minute at rest — reaching 20–25 L/min during intense exercise (MOORE; DALLEY; AGUR, 2014). This functional demand imposes a highly specialized architecture: walls of different thicknesses corresponding to the pressures generated, valves that open and close passively by pressure gradient, and a dedicated circulation independent of intracavitary blood.

Cardiac chambers

Fig. 1 — Internal anatomy — Frontal section of the heart: chambers, atrioventricular and semilunar valves. Gray's Anatomy, Henry Gray, 1918. Public domain.

The heart has four chambers: two atria (superior, thin-walled, receptive) and two ventricles (inferior, thick-walled, propulsive). The longitudinal division — the interatrial septum and the interventricular septum — completely separates the pulmonary and systemic circulations after birth. Any residual communication between the two sides represents a congenital malformation with hemodynamic consequences proportional to the shunt produced (MOORE; DALLEY; AGUR, 2014) (Fig. 490).RA (átrio direito) · RV (ventrículo direito) · LA (átrio esquerdo) · LV (ventrículo esquerdo) · SVC (veia cava superior) · IVC (veia cava inferior)

Right atrium (RA)

Receives systemic venous blood from the superior and inferior venae cavae, and blood from the heart itself via the coronary sinus (sinus coronarius). Its posterior wall is smooth (derived from the embryonic sinus venosus); the anterior wall and right atrial appendage (right auricle) are trabeculated by pectinate muscles (musculi pectinati). The boundary between the two regions is the crista terminalis — a vertical muscular ridge with clinical relevance as a site of origin of focal atrial tachycardias. On the interatrial septum lies the fossa ovalis, an oval depression corresponding to the closed fetal foramen ovale. In 20–25% of adults this fusion is incomplete — a patent foramen ovale (PFO — forame oval patente) — which is usually functionally sealed but can serve as a route for paradoxical embolism.

Right ventricle (RV)

A low-pressure chamber (systolic pressure ~25 mmHg), with thinner walls (3–5 mm) than the left ventricle. Its cross-sectional shape is crescent-like, partially wrapping around the LV. The interior is trabeculated by trabeculae carneae, among them the trabecula septomarginalis (moderator band), which carries the right bundle branch directly to the anterior papillary muscle — a shortcut that anticipates papillary muscle activation and prevents tricuspid regurgitation during systole (Fig. 490). The outflow tract — the infundibulum (conus arteriosus) — has smooth walls and directs flow toward the pulmonary valve.

Left atrium (LA)

Higher atrial pressure chamber (mean ~8–12 mmHg), receives oxygenated blood from all four pulmonary veins. Its posterior wall is almost entirely smooth. The left atrial appendage (left auricle) is tubular, with a narrow base — a morphology that promotes stasis and thrombus formation in atrial fibrillation, making it the primary site of intracardiac thrombus in AF. The interatrial septum shows, on the left side, the valve of the foramen ovale, which acts as a flap over the septum primum.

Left ventricle (LV)

A high-pressure chamber (systolic pressure ~120 mmHg), with walls 8–12 mm thick — roughly three times thicker than the RV. Its shape is conical, with a trabeculated inner surface. Two groups of papillary muscles — anterolateral and posteromedial — sustain the chordae tendineae (chordae tendineae) that anchor the mitral valve leaflets. Unlike the RV, the LV has geometrically separated inflow (mitral valve) and outflow (aortic valve) tracts, divided by the membranous interventricular septum and the anterior mitral leaflet. This architecture requires the LV contraction to be simultaneously centripetal and longitudinal, generating a normal ejection fraction (fração de ejeção) of 55–70% (Fig. 490).

Key concept — Wall thickness and pressure

Each chamber's wall thickness is proportional to the pressure it generates: the LV (~120 mmHg systolic) has walls ~3× thicker than the RV (~25 mmHg). Chronic pressure overload induces concentric hypertrophy (thicker walls, preserved cavity); volume overload induces eccentric hypertrophy (dilation with proportionally thin walls). This morphological distinction guides etiological diagnosis on echocardiography (GUYTON; HALL, 2021).

Cardiac valves

The four cardiac valves are passive structures — they open and close by pressure gradient, with no intrinsic musculature. They divide into atrioventricular valves (mitral and tricuspid), separating atria from ventricles, and semilunar valves (aortic and pulmonary), separating ventricles from the great arteries.

Tricuspid valve

Located at the right atrioventricular orifice (ostium atrioventriculare dextrum). Has three leaflets (cusps): anterior, posterior, and septal. The leaflets are anchored to the tricuspid annulus and to chordae tendineae attached to the anterior, posterior, and septal papillary muscles of the RV. The septal leaflet is closest to the membranous interventricular septum — a structure with the atrioventricular node immediately adjacent, which is why surgery on the tricuspid valve or defects of the membranous septum can cause AV block.

Mitral valve (bicuspid valve)

Located at the left atrioventricular orifice (ostium atrioventriculare sinistrum). Has two leaflets: anterior (larger, near the aortic valve) and posterior. The anterior leaflet is contiguous with the non-coronary cusp of the aortic valve — the mitroaortic continuity — which explains why degenerative or infectious disease can affect both simultaneously. Chordae tendineae connect the leaflets to the anterolateral and posteromedial papillary muscles; the posteromedial muscle has a single blood supply (right coronary artery or circumflex) and is more vulnerable to ischemia — the primary cause of acute ischemic mitral regurgitation (insuficiência mitral isquêmica aguda) (NETTER, 2019).

Pulmonary valve

Located at the RV outflow tract, in the infundibulum. Has three semilunar cusps: anterior, right, and left. It has no subvalvular apparatus (no chordae or papillary muscles) — closure is guaranteed solely by the pressure gradient between the pulmonary trunk and the RV during diastole. Pulmonary diastolic pressure (~10 mmHg) is low, which facilitates closure and makes pulmonary regurgitation hemodynamically tolerated for prolonged periods.

Aortic valve

Located at the LV outflow tract, with three semilunar cusps: right (right coronary), left (left coronary), and posterior (non-coronary). Between each cusp and the aortic wall are dilatations called sinuses of Valsalva (aortic sinuses); from the right and left sinuses the coronary arteries emerge. Coronary flow occurs predominantly during diastole, when the aortic valve is closed and the sinus creates a vortex that directs blood toward the coronary ostia — a passive and elegant mechanism that requires no accessory pump to perfuse the myocardium itself.

Great vessels

Plate 492 — Sternocostal surface: right ventricle, pulmonary trunk, ascending aorta and aortic arch. Gray's Anatomy, 1918. Public domain.

The great vessels are the largest-caliber arteries and veins directly connected to the heart. Their anatomy determines both normal physiology and surgical pathology, as well as the basis for catheterization and hemodynamic procedures.RV (ventrículo direito) · LV (ventrículo esquerdo) · Pulmonary trunk (tronco pulmonar) · Aorta (aorta) · Right auricle (aurícula direita)

Aorta

The largest artery in the body, arising from the LV. It is divided into: ascending aorta (from the aortic valve to the aortic arch, ~5 cm; normal diameter ≤40 mm); aortic arch, from which arise the brachiocephalic trunk, the left common carotid artery, and the left subclavian artery; descending thoracic aorta, posterior to the pericardium; and the abdominal aorta. The aortic wall is composed of three layers — intima, media (rich in elastic fibers), and adventitia. Aortic dissection occurs when blood enters the media through an intimal tear, creating a false lumen. The location of the dissection relative to the coronaries and aortic valve determines surgical urgency (Stanford classification type A vs. type B) (MOORE; DALLEY; AGUR, 2014) (Fig. 492).

Pulmonary trunk and pulmonary arteries

The pulmonary trunk (truncus pulmonalis) arises from the RV, runs ~5 cm, and bifurcates into the right and left pulmonary arteries, beneath the aortic arch. It is the only arterial vessel in the adult body that carries venous (deoxygenated) blood. The arterial ligament (ligamentum arteriosum) — the fibrous remnant of the fetal ductus arteriosus — connects the upper surface of the pulmonary trunk to the descending aorta; its persistence after birth (patent ductus arteriosus — PDA (persistência do canal arterial — PCA)) produces a left-to-right shunt (Fig. 492).

Venae cavae

The superior vena cava (SVC) drains the head, neck, upper limbs, and thorax into the RA; the inferior vena cava (IVC) drains the abdomen and lower limbs. Both open into the RA without valves. Extrinsic compression of the SVC by mediastinal masses causes superior vena cava syndrome (síndrome da veia cava superior) (cape edema, facial cyanosis, thoracic collateral circulation).

Pulmonary veins

Four pulmonary veins — two right and two left — carry oxygenated blood from the lungs to the LA. They are the only venous vessels in the body that carry arterialized blood. Their ostia in the LA are relevant in catheter ablation for AF: the pulmonary veins are the origin of most ectopic foci that trigger and perpetuate atrial fibrillation, which is why electrical isolation of these ostia forms the basis of the procedure.

Coronary circulation

Blausen 0256 — Coronary arteries (artérias coronárias). Aorta · Left Main CA (coronária esquerda principal) · LAD (descendente anterior esquerda) · Circumflex (circunflexa) · RCA (coronária direita) · PDA (descendente posterior)

Left coronary artery (LCA)

Arises from the left coronary sinus of the aorta, runs 1–2 cm as the left main coronary artery (LMCA) and bifurcates into: left anterior descending artery (LAD) — descends along the anterior interventricular sulcus (sulcus interventricularis anterior), giving off diagonal branches (anterior LV wall) and septal perforating branches (anterior two-thirds of the septum); and left circumflex artery (LCx) — runs along the left atrioventricular sulcus (sulcus atrioventricularis), giving off obtuse marginal branches (lateral LV wall). The LAD is the most frequently affected artery in myocardial infarction (infarto agudo do miocárdio — IAM); its proximal occlusion is called the "widow maker" because it interrupts the supply to the largest myocardial mass (GRAY; STANDRING, 2021) (Fig. 495).

Right coronary artery (RCA)

Arises from the right coronary sinus, descends along the right atrioventricular sulcus giving off branches to the RV, and at the crux cordis (intersection of the posterior interventricular and atrioventricular sulci) gives rise to the posterior descending artery (PDA) in most individuals — a configuration called right dominance (~70% of cases). The RCA also supplies the sinoatrial node (~60%) and the atrioventricular node (~90%), explaining why inferior infarction (RCA occlusion) frequently presents with transient AV block (bloqueio atrioventricular transitório) (Fig. 495).

Coronary dominance

The concept of dominance refers to which artery gives rise to the PDA and branches to the AV node: right (~70%), left (~15%), or codominance (~15%). Dominance has prognostic relevance in myocardial infarction: occlusion of the dominant artery compromises a larger myocardial mass and conduction territory.

Coronary venous drainage

Venous blood from the myocardium drains predominantly through the coronary sinus (in the posterior atrioventricular sulcus), which empties into the RA. The cardiac veins accompany the arteries: the great cardiac vein (accompanies LAD and LCx), the middle cardiac vein (accompanies PDA), and the small cardiac vein (accompanies RCA). A small fraction drains directly into the chambers through the Thebesian veins (venae cordis minimae) — minimal communications of limited clinical significance.

Key concept — Diastolic coronary flow

The myocardium contracts during systole, compressing the intramural vessels and nearly interrupting coronary flow during that phase. Coronary supply occurs predominantly during diastole, when the muscle relaxes. This explains why severe tachycardia (short diastole) reduces coronary perfusion, and why aortic diastolic pressure is the primary determinant of coronary perfusion: low diastolic BP = reduced perfusion gradient. This principle is central to understanding cardiogenic shock, severe aortic stenosis, and the use of vasopressors in the management of acute MI (GUYTON; HALL, 2021).

Electrical conduction system

Plate (internal structure) — Internal structure: septum, valves, and conduction system. Gray's Anatomy, 1918. Public domain.

The heart has a specialized system for generating and conducting electrical impulses that ensures coordinated contraction sequence — atria first, ventricles after — with a calculated delay that allows ventricular filling. This system is composed of modified cells with intrinsic automaticity (spontaneous depolarization) and variable conduction velocity.SA node (nó sinoatrial) · AV node (nó atrioventricular) · Bundle of His (feixe de His) · Purkinje fibers (fibras de Purkinje)

Sinoatrial node (SA node)

The physiological pacemaker, located at the junction of the SVC and the RA, near the crista terminalis. Fires at 60–100 bpm at rest. Innervated by the sympathetic nervous system (increases rate) and the vagus nerve (decreases rate). Supplied by the SA nodal artery, a branch of the RCA in ~60% and of the LCx in ~40%. SA node dysfunction produces sick sinus syndrome (síndrome do nó doente — bradicardia sinusal, pausas, síndrome bradi-taqui) (Fig. 498).

Internodal and interatrial pathways

The impulse propagates through the atrial myocardium from cell to cell. Preferential internodal conduction pathways exist (anterior, middle, and posterior — Bachmann's bundle), although the concept of "anatomically distinct internodal pathways" is debated — the anatomical substrate is oriented atrial myocardium, not isolated specialized tissue. Bachmann's bundle (fasciculus interatrialis) conducts the impulse to the LA, synchronizing bilateral atrial activation.

Atrioventricular node (AV node)

Located in the triangle of Koch (trigonum Koch) — bounded by the coronary sinus ostium, the septal leaflet of the tricuspid valve, and the tendon of Todaro (Fig. 498). It is the only normal conduction point between atria and ventricles (the remaining are separated by the fibrous rings of the cardiac skeleton). The AV node imposes a physiological delay of 120–200 ms (visible as the PR interval on the ECG), allowing ventricular filling. Intrinsic escape rate: 40–60 bpm. Dual blood supply (RCA + LCx) provides relative protection, but inferior ischemia can compromise it.

Bundle of His and bundle branches

From the AV node, the impulse travels through the bundle of His (fasciculus atrioventricularis) — the sole conduction pathway through the membranous septum — and divides into the right bundle branch (thin, runs through the septum to the moderator band → RV) and the left bundle branch (broad, divides into anterosuperior and posteroinferior fascicles → LV). The left bundle branch is more vulnerable to ischemia and fibrosis due to its larger territory. Left bundle branch block (LBBB — bloqueio de ramo esquerdo) carries greater prognostic significance than isolated right bundle branch block (RBBB — bloqueio de ramo direito) (Fig. 498).

Purkinje fibers

The bundle branches terminate in a network of Purkinje fibers — large-diameter cells with high conduction velocity (2–4 m/s, ~6× faster than ordinary ventricular myocardium). The subendocardial network ensures near-simultaneous activation of the entire ventricular mass, from apex toward base. Intrinsic firing rate: 20–40 bpm (last-resort pacemaker when SA and AV nodes fail).

Pericardium

The pericardium (pericardium) is the fibroserious sac enclosing the heart and the roots of the great vessels. It is composed of two layers: the fibrous pericardium (dense connective tissue, inextensible, fused with the diaphragm inferiorly and with the adventitia of the great vessels superiorly) and the serous pericardium, subdivided into a parietal layer (lines the inner surface of the fibrous pericardium) and a visceral layer — the epicardium — which adheres to the myocardium.

The pericardial space between the two serous layers contains 15–50 mL of lubricating fluid. Pathological accumulation of this fluid is a pericardial effusion (derrame pericárdico). The rigidity of the fibrous pericardium is decisive: rapid accumulation of 150–200 mL can raise intrapericardial pressure enough to compress the right-sided chambers (cardiac tamponade), while slow accumulation can reach 1–2 L without hemodynamic collapse, because the fibrous pericardium adapts progressively.

Sensory innervation of the parietal pericardium is provided by the phrenic nerve (C3–C5), explaining pericardial pain referred to the shoulder and cervical region — and hiccups as a manifestation of pericardial irritation. The visceral layer has no sensory innervation.

Topography and projection on the thoracic wall

Plate 493 — Diaphragmatic surface and topography of the heart in the mediastinum. Gray's Anatomy, 1918. Public domain.

The heart occupies the middle mediastinum, situated between the 2nd and 6th anterior ribs, with 2/3 to the left of the midline and 1/3 to the right. Its anatomical axis is oblique: the base points upward, rightward, and posteriorly; the apex points downward, leftward, and anteriorly.RA border (borda atrial direita) · LV border (borda ventricular esquerda) · Apex (ápice) · Base (base)

The four chambers have distinct topographic relationships with the thoracic wall, explaining the classical cardiac auscultation areas:

  • Right cardiac border: formed by the RA (from the 3rd to the 6th right intercostal space, ~2 cm lateral to the sternum).
  • Superior border: formed by the pulmonary trunk (left) and ascending aorta (right), at the level of the 2nd rib.
  • Left border: formed by the LV (inferior) and the left atrial appendage (superior), from the 2nd to the 5th left rib.
  • Inferior border (diaphragmatic): formed by the RV (right 2/3) and LV (left 1/3), resting on the diaphragm.

The cardiac impulse (apical impulse) is the pulsation of the LV apex palpable at the 5th left intercostal space, at the midclavicular line (Fig. 493). Its lateral or inferolateral displacement indicates cardiomegaly. Valve projections and auscultation areas diverge: sound produced by a valve is transmitted in the direction of blood flow, which is why the aortic valve (located behind the sternum, 3rd space) is best heard at the 2nd right intercostal space.

The epicardium is the visceral layer of the serous pericardium. It contains subepicardial adipose tissue in which the coronary arteries, veins, and nerves course. Epicardial adipose tissue has its own metabolic and inflammatory activity, with an emerging role in the pathogenesis of coronary artery disease and AF.

The myocardium is the muscular layer responsible for contractile function. It is composed of cardiomyocytes — striated muscle cells, typically with a single central nucleus, interconnected by intercalated discs (disci intercalares) that ensure rapid cell-to-cell electrical conduction (functional syncytium). Ventricular myocardium is organized in helical fiber layers with different orientations, enabling the systolic torsion that increases ejection efficiency. → Cardiac cycle: physiology of contraction.

The endocardium is the inner layer of endothelium and subendothelial connective tissue, lining the chambers and covering the valves. It is continuous with the vascular endothelium. Endocardial injury (e.g., by high-velocity regurgitant jets in valvular disease) creates thrombogenic surfaces — the substrate for infective endocarditis (endocardite infecciosa).

The heart has five surfaces: sternocostal (anterior — predominantly RV), diaphragmatic (inferior — RV and LV), right pulmonary, left pulmonary, and base (posterior — LA). Knowledge of the surfaces is important for correlating infarction territory with ECG lead: anterior wall infarction correlates with leads V1–V4 (LAD territory); inferior infarction with leads II, III, aVF (RCA); lateral infarction with I, aVL, V5–V6 (LCx or diagonal).

Historical context

2nd century AD Galen of Pergamon describes the heart as the source of vital heat and pneuma. He incorrectly postulated pores in the interventricular septum allowing direct passage of blood between ventricles — a view that persisted for 1,400 years.
1628 William Harvey publishes Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis, demonstrating experimentally that blood circulation is a closed system and the heart is a pump — the first quantitative and mechanistic description of cardiac function.
1706 Raymond Vieussens provides detailed anatomical description of the coronary arteries, establishing the basis for understanding coronary circulation.
1893 Wilhelm His Jr. describes the bundle of His — the first anatomically identified component of the specialized conduction system.
1907 Arthur Keith and Martin Flack identify the sinoatrial node — the cardiac pacemaker — in dissections of mammalian hearts.
1909 Sunao Tawara publishes a complete description of the conduction system, including the AV node, bundle of His, and bundle branches, establishing the anatomy of cardiac electrical conduction.
1929 Werner Forssmann performs the first cardiac catheterization on himself, inserting a catheter through the antecubital vein into the RA — the foundation of all modern hemodynamics and interventional cardiology.

Open questions

Open question

What is the exact contribution of epicardial adipose tissue to the pathogenesis of atrial fibrillation and coronary artery disease? Recent studies suggest an inflammatory and paracrine role, but the molecular mechanisms and causal directionality remain poorly established.

Open question

Are the internodal pathways (Bachmann's bundle, Wenckebach's bundle, Thorel's pathway) anatomically distinct and isolated structures, or do they merely reflect preferential orientation of the atrial myocardium? The question has direct implications for catheter ablation of atrial arrhythmias.

Open question

What is the true regenerative potential of adult myocardium? The existence of resident cardiac stem cells and their capacity to regenerate after infarction remains controversial — claims of significant regeneration were refuted following scientific fraud (the Anversa case) but have not been entirely resolved biologically.

Open question

How does coronary dominance variation interact with risk factors to determine the pattern and severity of myocardial infarction? Left dominance appears associated with worse prognosis in ST-elevation MI, but the mechanisms underlying this asymmetry are still debated.

Discussion

The motivating question — why a coronary laceration kills within minutes while an equivalent arterial injury elsewhere allows hours for intervention — finds its answer in the convergence of three anatomically unique characteristics of the heart: (1) the myocardium already extracts near-maximal oxygen from coronary blood at rest (~70% extraction, versus ~25% in skeletal muscle), leaving no extraction reserve to compensate for reduced flow; (2) the absence of functionally significant coronary anastomoses in most individuals prevents immediate collateral circulation; and (3) coronary flow is diastolic, and any compensatory tachycardia shortening diastole worsens ischemia at the very moment the heart most needs oxygen.

There is a profound tension in cardiac anatomy between robustness and vulnerability: the heart is the only organ that cannot stop functioning even for a fraction of a second to be supplied, and paradoxically is the only one that interrupts its own supply with every systole. The evolutionary solution — diastolic flow through the sinuses of Valsalva — is elegant but creates dependence on aortic diastolic pressure and diastolic duration. The entire edifice of coronary pathology, from cardiogenic shock to transmural infarction, is a logical consequence of this anatomical equation.

An implication that emerges from the data but is rarely stated explicitly: the anatomy of the cardiac surfaces and coronary territories creates a map of predictability that transforms the ECG into an instrument of anatomical localization before it is a diagnostic instrument. The lead showing ST elevation is the electrical window into the ischemic surface; the ischemic surface belongs to a specific coronary territory; the territory identifies the culprit vessel by its occlusion pattern. Anatomy, electrophysiology, and clinical medicine are, in this field, a single language.

Physics

Guiding question

A handful of equations — few enough to fit on a T-shirt — describe everything from a falling apple to black holes colliding billions of light-years away. Why does nature let itself be written in mathematics, and how far does that alphabet reach?

What physics studies ¶

Physics (from the Greek phýsis, nature) is the science that seeks the most fundamental regularities of nature — the laws governing matter, energy, space, and time, and the interactions among them. Its defining bet is that behind the variety of phenomena lie a few general principles, expressed in mathematical language, from which the rest can in principle be derived. It is the science that asks not what exists in a particular case, but how everything behaves at the most basic level we can reach.

What sets physics apart from the other natural sciences is the degree of abstraction and unification it pursues. Chemistry studies how atoms combine; biology, how matter organizes itself into living things. Physics sits one rung below: it asks what an atom is, what forces hold it together, and why energy is conserved in every reaction. In principle, chemistry and biology are physics applied to complex systems — though, in practice, each level of organization has its own laws that do not read off directly from the particles (the problem of emergence).

Physics works through idealized models and through scales. No equation describes a real object in every detail; the physicist isolates the essential (a "point mass" with no size, an "ideal gas" with no interactions) and measures how far the idealization strays from reality. And every theory has a domain of validity: Newton's mechanics describes apples and planets with extraordinary precision, but fails near the speed of light (where relativity takes over) and at the scale of atoms (where quantum mechanics does). Knowing the limits of each theory is as much a part of physics as knowing its formulas.

The ruler of scales

Physics spans more than 60 orders of magnitude in size: from the Planck length (~10−35 m, where the very notion of space may dissolve) to the diameter of the observable universe (~1027 m). And about 40 orders of magnitude in energy, from low-temperature physics to the highest-energy cosmic rays. Different theories reign over different stretches of that ruler — and one of the great projects of physics is to find out whether they are limiting cases of a single theory.

There is an important internal division of labor. Theoretical physics builds and develops the mathematical models; experimental physics puts them to the test, measuring with ever-growing precision. Neither stands alone: a theory that makes no testable predictions is not physics, and a measurement with no theory to interpret it is just a number. The cycle between the two — prediction, measurement, discrepancy, revision — is the engine of the field.

Map of the field ¶

Physics is usually divided into nine great territories. A cross-cutting divide runs through all of them: classical physics (up to about 1900 — mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, optics), which treats quantities as continuous and the world as deterministic; and modern physics (twentieth century onward — relativity and quantum theory), which rewrote the foundations upon discovering that spacetime is malleable and that matter is granular and probabilistic. The cards below give the overview; each subfield is developed in its own section just ahead.

Classical mechanics

Motion and forces in macroscopic, slow-moving objects. Newton's laws; the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulations; celestial mechanics. The base everything was built on.

Thermodynamics and statistics

Heat, energy, entropy, and temperature. The four laws; Boltzmann's statistical bridge between the world of molecules and macroscopic quantities.

Electromagnetism

Charges, currents, electric and magnetic fields. Maxwell's four equations unify electricity, magnetism, and light into a single phenomenon.

Optics and waves

Propagation, interference, diffraction, and polarization. Light as a wave, and the physics common to all wave phenomena, from sound to the sea.

Relativity

Special: relative space and time, E = mc2. General: gravity as the curvature of spacetime. The theory of the very fast and the very massive.

Quantum mechanics

The atomic and subatomic world: quantization, wave-particle duality, uncertainty, superposition. Deterministic in its probabilities, not in its outcomes.

Particles and fields

The ultimate constituents of matter and their interactions. The Standard Model, quarks and leptons, the fundamental forces, and the Higgs field.

Condensed matter

How billions of atoms together give rise to new properties: solids, semiconductors, superconductivity, phase transitions. The physics of technology.

Astrophysics and cosmology

Stars, galaxies, and the universe as a whole — its origin in the Big Bang, its expansion, and the puzzles of dark matter and dark energy.

Classical mechanics ¶

Classical mechanics describes how objects move under the action of forces, in the regime of everyday speeds and sizes larger than the atom. It is the oldest and most intuitive of the physical theories — and still the one that designs bridges, launches rockets, and predicts eclipses.

At its core are Newton's three laws (1687). The first (inertia) states that a body keeps its state of rest or uniform straight-line motion unless a net force acts on it — rest is not the "natural" state; constant motion is just as natural. The second quantifies how a force changes that motion, and is the central equation of all mechanics:

F = m · anet force (N) = mass (kg) × acceleration (m/s²)

The third law (action and reaction) states that forces always come in pairs: if A pushes B, B pushes A with an equal and opposite force. From it follows the conservation of linear momentum (p = m·v), one of the quantities physics found are never created or destroyed in an isolated system — only transferred.

Universal gravitation was the theory's inaugural triumph. Newton showed that the same force that makes an apple fall keeps the Moon in orbit, with a single law: the attraction between two masses grows with their product and falls off with the square of the distance.

F = G · m1m2 / r2G ≈ 6.674 × 10−11 N·m²/kg² (gravitational constant)

With it, Kepler's three laws of planetary motion — previously empirical rules extracted from observation — became derivable consequences. That was the founding moment of the program of physics: reducing many disparate facts to a single, calculable principle. To situate the scale: the same equation describes the fall of an object (g ≈ 9.8 m/s² on Earth) and the orbital period of Neptune (165 years).

Two centuries later, mechanics gained deeper reformulations, mathematically equivalent to Newton but more powerful. The Lagrangian formulation (Lagrange, 1788) describes motion via a principle of least action: of all possible paths between two points, nature picks the one that minimizes a quantity called the action. The Hamiltonian formulation (Hamilton, 1833) recasts everything in terms of energy and is the bridge through which quantum mechanics would later be built. They are not "another physics" — they are the same mechanics seen from an angle that reveals its symmetries and extends naturally to systems Newton could not reach.

Symmetry and conservation (Noether's theorem, 1918)

Emmy Noether proved one of the most beautiful results in physics: to every continuous symmetry of nature there corresponds a conservation law. Because the laws do not change over time, energy is conserved; because they do not change from place to place, momentum is conserved; because they do not change with orientation, angular momentum is conserved. "Conservation laws" cease to be brute facts and become reflections of the structure of space and time.

Classical mechanics also revealed its own limits of predictability. Even though it is deterministic — given the initial state, the future is fixed — systems with three or more interacting bodies can be chaotic: a tiny difference in the initial conditions grows exponentially, making long-term prediction impossible in practice. Weather and the long-term orbit of the Solar System are examples. Chaos showed that determinism does not imply predictability.

Thermodynamics and statistical mechanics ¶

Thermodynamics was born in the nineteenth century from a practical question — how to extract work from heat, the problem of steam engines — and ended up revealing principles that hold for the entire universe. It deals with heat, temperature, energy, and entropy, and does so without needing to know what matter is made of: these are laws about macroscopic quantities.

Its laws are four. The zeroth law grounds the notion of temperature: if two bodies are in thermal equilibrium with a third, they are in equilibrium with each other. The first law is the conservation of energy with heat included: the internal energy of a system changes by the heat it receives minus the work it does.

ΔU = Q − Wchange in internal energy = heat received − work done

The second law is the deepest and the strangest. It states that the entropy (a measure of disorder) of an isolated system never decreases: natural processes have a preferred direction. Heat flows from hot to cold, never spontaneously the reverse; a glass shatters, but shards do not reassemble on their own. It is the second law that defines the arrow of time — the only fundamental law that distinguishes past from future.

The third law states that it is impossible to reach absolute zero (0 K = −273.15 °C) in a finite number of steps; as it is approached, the entropy of a perfect crystal tends to a minimum value.

Boltzmann's bridge: entropy is counting

Statistical mechanics (Boltzmann, Gibbs, late nineteenth century) explained why thermodynamics works, linking the macro to the micro. Its summary equation, engraved on Boltzmann's tombstone, defines entropy as the logarithm of the number of microscopic arrangements (W) compatible with the observed macroscopic state:

S = kB · ln WkB ≈ 1.38 × 10−23 J/K (Boltzmann constant)

Entropy thus measures the number of microscopic ways to realize the same macroscopic state. Heat flows from hot to cold not because of a force, but because there are overwhelmingly more arrangements corresponding to the mixed state than to the separated one. The second law becomes a statement about probability — the improbable is not impossible, merely fantastically rare.

This statistical rereading has a philosophical consequence that still unsettles: the microscopic laws (Newton's, Maxwell's) are time-reversible — they do not distinguish past from future — yet macroscopic behavior is irreversible. How does irreversibility emerge from reversible laws? This is Boltzmann's problem, tied to the hypothesis that the universe began in a state of extraordinarily low entropy (see Active controversies).

Thermodynamics also governs phase transitions — when water turns to ice or steam, when a metal becomes magnetic. Near the critical point of these transitions, physically very different systems behave identically (universality), a phenomenon whose explanation — Kenneth Wilson's renormalization group (1971) — connects thermodynamics to particle physics.

Electromagnetism ¶

Electromagnetism is the theory of one of the four fundamental forces — the one responsible for practically everything we experience beyond gravity: light, chemistry, the cohesion of solid matter, all of electronics. Its history is the canonical example of unification in physics: phenomena taken to be separate — electricity, magnetism, and light — turned out to be three faces of a single field.

In the early nineteenth century, electricity and magnetism were distinct studies. Then Oersted (1820) noticed that an electric current deflects a compass needle — current generates magnetism — and Faraday (1831) discovered the reverse: a moving magnet generates a current (electromagnetic induction, the principle of every generator and transformer). Faraday introduced the decisive idea of the field: the force does not act at a distance across empty space, but through an entity that fills space and transmits the interaction.

It was James Clerk Maxwell (1865) who completed the synthesis, gathering everything into four equations. They say, in words: (1) electric charges create electric fields; (2) there are no isolated "magnetic charges" — every magnet has two poles; (3) changing magnetic fields generate electric fields (Faraday); and (4) currents and changing electric fields generate magnetic fields. It was the term Maxwell added to the fourth equation that closed the loop.

Light is an electromagnetic wave

By combining his equations, Maxwell obtained a wave: a changing electric field generates a changing magnetic field, which regenerates the electric one, so the disturbance propagates on its own through the vacuum. The speed of that wave came out of the equations from two constants measured in the lab — and it matched the speed of light. The conclusion was inescapable: light is an electromagnetic wave. Radio, microwaves, infrared, visible light, X-rays, and gamma rays are the same thing at different frequencies.

c = 299 792 458 m/sspeed of light in vacuum — today a defined value that fixes the metre

Maxwell's equations had a consequence that only relativity would resolve: they give one value for the speed of light, without saying "relative to what." If light always travels at the same speed, regardless of who measures it, then the Newtonian physics of adding velocities cannot be entirely right. That tension was Einstein's starting point (see Relativity).

Electromagnetism is also the force that structures matter: it is electric forces that bind electrons to nuclei, atoms into molecules, and molecules into solids. When you touch a table, what you feel is the electromagnetic repulsion between the electrons in your hand and those in the table — there is never any "real" contact between surfaces. All of chemistry is electromagnetism plus quantum mechanics.

Optics and waves ¶

A wave is a disturbance that carries energy (and information) without carrying matter: the cork bobs up and down, but the sea wave moves forward. The study of waves is transversal to all of physics — sound, light, seismic waves, waves on a guitar string, and even gravitational waves obey the same relations. Every wave has a wavelength (λ, the distance between two crests), a frequency (f, how many crests pass per second), and a speed, linked by a simple relation:

v = λ · fspeed = wavelength × frequency

Optics is the specific study of light. At scales large compared to the wavelength, light behaves as if it traveled in straight rays — this is geometrical optics, which explains mirrors, lenses, the eye, the camera, and the rainbow through refraction (the bending when changing medium) and reflection. It is the optics of high-school classes, and it suffices to design most instruments.

But light reveals its wave nature in two phenomena that rays cannot explain. Interference: two overlapping waves can add up (coinciding crests) or cancel out (crest over trough). Thomas Young's double-slit experiment (1801) — light through two slits produces alternating bright and dark fringes — proved that light is a wave, closing (for a century) the debate with Newton's corpuscular theory. Diffraction: waves bend around obstacles and spread out on passing through openings, the more so the smaller the opening is relative to λ.

Polarization further reveals that light is a transverse wave — it oscillates perpendicular to the direction of propagation — and that this oscillation has an orientation, which polarizing filters (sunglasses, LCD screens) select. Sound, being a longitudinal wave (it oscillates along the direction of propagation), cannot be polarized — a difference that distinguishes the two kinds of wave.

Where the regimes meet: geometrical optics is the limit of wave optics when the wavelength is negligible compared to the objects — just as Newton's mechanics is the limit of relativity at low speeds. And wave optics itself would turn out, in the twentieth century, to be a limit too: light is also made of photons, packets of energy. Wave or particle, depending on the experiment — the paradox that quantum mechanics would have to face head-on.

A universal wave phenomenon is the Doppler effect: frequency rises when the source approaches and falls when it recedes (the siren that changes pitch as it passes). Applied to the light of galaxies, it becomes the redshift that revealed the expansion of the universe (see Astrophysics and cosmology) — an example of how a concept from waves runs through all of physics.

Relativity ¶

Relativity, Einstein's work in two acts (1905 and 1915), remade the physics of space and time. Where Newton treated them as a fixed, universal stage — the same clock and ruler for everyone — Einstein showed that both are relative to the observer and to motion, and that gravity is a property of the geometry of the universe itself.

Special relativity (1905) starts from two simple postulates: (1) the laws of physics are the same for all observers in uniform motion; and (2) the speed of light in vacuum is the same for everyone, no matter how they move — exactly what Maxwell's equations required. From these two premises follow consequences that defy common sense, all experimentally confirmed: moving clocks run slower (time dilation), moving objects shorten along the direction of motion (length contraction), and simultaneity is relative — two events simultaneous for me may not be for you.

From the same formalism comes the most famous equation in science, stating that mass and energy are the same thing in different units:

E = m · c2a mass m is equivalent to an energy E — the factor c2 is enormous

Because c2 is a gigantic number, a tiny amount of mass holds an immense energy: it is the source of the Sun's energy (nuclear fusion) and of nuclear power plants and bombs. Newton's mechanics reappears here as the low-speed limit of relativity — for v much smaller than c, the relativistic corrections vanish and we recover F = m·a. No theory "overthrows" the previous one; it contains it as a special case.

General relativity: gravity is geometry

In 1915, Einstein extended the theory to include gravity and acceleration. His central idea: mass and energy curve spacetime, and that curvature is what we call gravity. The Earth does not "pull" the Moon by an invisible force — it deforms the spacetime around it, and the Moon follows the straightest possible path through that curved geometry. As John Wheeler put it: "matter tells spacetime how to curve; spacetime tells matter how to move."

General relativity made extraordinary predictions, all confirmed: light bends when passing near the Sun (measured in the 1919 eclipse, which made Einstein famous), time passes more slowly in strong gravitational fields (the GPS must correct for this to work), and spacetime itself can ripple. In 2015, the LIGO observatory directly detected gravitational waves — ripples in spacetime produced by the collision of two black holes more than a billion light-years away — a century after the theory predicted them.

The theory also predicts its own frontiers. Where the curvature becomes extreme — at the center of a black hole or at the initial instant of the Big Bang — it points to singularities: points of infinite density where the equations stop making sense. This is read as a sign that relativity, so successful on the large scale, must be replaced by a quantum theory of gravity on the very small scale (see Open questions).

Quantum mechanics ¶

Quantum mechanics is the theory of the very small — atoms, electrons, photons — and the most precise and best-tested theory in the history of physics. It is also the most counterintuitive: in the quantum domain, matter is granular, chance is fundamental, and objects can be in several states at once. No image from everyday life applies; what remains is the mathematics, which works with astonishing precision.

It was born of an impasse. In 1900, to explain the radiation of hot bodies, Planck was forced to assume that energy is not emitted continuously, but in discrete packets (quanta). In 1905, Einstein applied the idea to light (the photoelectric effect, which earned him the Nobel): light is made of photons, each with energy proportional to its frequency.

E = h · fh ≈ 6.626 × 10−34 J·s (Planck constant) — the "grain" of action

From there came the first great puzzle: wave-particle duality. Light, which Young had proved to be a wave, is also a particle; and De Broglie (1924) showed that the reverse holds — electrons, which are particles, also diffract like waves. In the double-slit experiment done with electrons one at a time, each electron arrives as a point (particle), yet the accumulated pattern is one of interference (wave). The quantum object is neither wave nor particle: it is something new, which manifests one way or the other depending on the question the experiment asks.

The state of a quantum system is described by a wave function (ψ), which evolves perfectly deterministically through the Schrödinger equation (1926). What the wave function provides, however, is not the outcome of a measurement, but the probability of each possible outcome (Born rule: the probability is |ψ|²). Before measurement, the system is in a superposition of all outcomes; the measurement returns just one, apparently at random.

The uncertainty principle (Heisenberg, 1927)

It is not a limitation of the instruments, but of nature: certain pairs of quantities — such as position and momentum — do not have simultaneously definite values. The more precise a particle's position, the more indefinite its momentum, and vice versa:

Δx · Δp ≥ ħ/2ħ = h/2π — the product of the uncertainties has an irreducible floor

The quantum particle simply does not possess an exact position and momentum at the same time. It is one of the most radical breaks with classical physics, where everything had precise values, even when unknown.

Quantum theory also introduced spin (an intrinsic angular momentum with no classical analogue) and entanglement (emaranhamento): two particles can become correlated in such a way that measuring one instantly determines the state of the other, however far apart they are. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance" and refused to accept it; Bell's experiments (from the 1970s onward, awarded the 2022 Nobel) proved quantum theory right and Einstein wrong — entanglement is real. It is now the resource behind quantum computers and quantum cryptography.

The relation to classical physics is the correspondence principle: for large quantum numbers, or when the Planck constant is negligible compared to the quantities involved, the quantum predictions converge to the classical ones. The macroscopic world is quantum "underneath," but granularity and superposition become imperceptible at our scale — partly because of decoherence, the rapid scrambling of superpositions by interaction with the environment. What the wave function actually represents remains an open dispute (see Active controversies).

Particle physics and quantum field theory ¶

Particle physics seeks the ultimate constituents of matter and the rules of their interactions. Its language is quantum field theory (QFT), which unifies quantum mechanics with special relativity: in it, each kind of particle is an excitation of a field that fills all of space — the electron is a vibration of the electron field, the photon of the electromagnetic field, and so on.

The consolidated result is the Standard Model, built between the 1960s and 1970s and verified with remarkable precision ever since. It organizes all known matter into two groups of fundamental particles: the quarks (six types; they combine into protons and neutrons) and the leptons (six types, including the electron and the neutrinos). The interactions among them are mediated by messenger particles (bosons): the photon carries the electromagnetic force, gluons the strong force, and the W and Z bosons the weak force.

The Higgs field (predicted 1964, confirmed 2012)

The Standard Model is only consistent if there is an extra field permeating space — the Higgs field — whose interaction with particles gives them mass. Particles that interact with it more are "heavier"; the photon, which does not interact, has no mass and travels at the speed of light. In 2012, the LHC (the great collider at CERN) detected the Higgs boson, the excitation of that field, completing the Standard Model's list of particles nearly half a century after the prediction by Peter Higgs and others.

The four fundamental forces are the skeleton of all of physics. Three of them are described by the Standard Model in quantum language; gravity, the most familiar, is precisely the one that resists being quantized — the great gap in theoretical physics.

Note the paradox of the strengths: gravity is trillions of trillions of times weaker than the others — a fridge magnet beats the gravitational pull of the entire planet on a paperclip. It dominates only on large scales because, unlike the electric force (in which opposite charges cancel), gravity always attracts and accumulates with mass. Explaining why it is so weak (the "hierarchy problem") is one of the open questions that motivated theories beyond the Standard Model, such as supersymmetry.

Despite its success, the Standard Model is admittedly incomplete: it does not include gravity, does not explain dark matter or dark energy, does not say why there is more matter than antimatter in the universe, and treats neutrinos as massless (though we know they have a little mass). It is the most successful theory we have and, at the same time, the clearest evidence that there is physics left to discover.

Condensed matter ¶

Condensed matter physics studies what happens when a vast number of atoms come together — solids and liquids, above all. It is the largest subfield of physics by number of researchers and the closest to technology: transistors, lasers, LEDs, magnets, batteries, and superconducting materials come from here. Its guiding principle is emergence: properties that no isolated atom has arise from the collective behavior of many.

The physicist Philip Anderson summed up this idea in 1972 in the phrase "more is different": knowing the equation of one atom does not let you predict superconductivity, just as knowing the biology of one neuron does not explain consciousness. Each level of complexity has its own laws, not reducible in practice to those of the level below — a thesis that repositions particle physics as not being "more fundamental" than the others in every sense.

The foundation is the crystal structure: in solids, atoms arrange themselves in regular periodic patterns. Quantum mechanics applied to that arrangement shows that electrons can only occupy certain bands of energy, separated by forbidden gaps. The width of that gap decides everything: if it is easy to cross, the material conducts electricity (conductor); if it is large, it insulates (insulator); if it is intermediate and controllable, it is a semiconductor — silicon — the basis of all electronics and of the digital revolution.

Superconductivity

Below a critical temperature, certain materials lose all electrical resistance: a current flows indefinitely without losing energy, and the material expels magnetic fields (allowing levitation). Discovered in 1911, it was only explained in 1957 (BCS theory): electrons, which normally repel each other, form pairs that move in perfect coordination, without colliding with the lattice. High-temperature superconductivity, discovered in 1986, still has no complete theoretical explanation — one of the greatest open problems in condensed matter, with enormous practical appeal (lossless power transmission, powerful magnets).

Condensed matter is also the stage where phase transitions and the concept of spontaneous symmetry breaking appear tangibly: a magnetic material, upon cooling, "chooses" a direction for its spins that did not exist before. That same mechanism, transposed to particle physics, is what the Higgs field accomplishes — one of the deepest conceptual bridges between the two subfields, and the reason Brazil invests in a synchrotron light source like Sirius to probe materials in atomic detail.

Astrophysics and cosmology ¶

Astrophysics applies the laws of physics to the objects of the sky — stars, galaxies, black holes — and cosmology deals with the universe as a whole: its origin, structure, and fate. It is the subfield where all the others meet, since the cosmos is the only laboratory with energies, densities, and timescales that no earthbound experiment can reach.

Stars are nuclear-fusion reactors in balance: gravity compresses them, and the energy released by the fusion of hydrogen into helium (strong force + relativity, via E = mc2) keeps them from collapsing. When the fuel runs out, that balance breaks and the star dies according to its mass: small ones become white dwarfs; large ones explode as supernovae, forging in their final instants the heavy elements we are made of, and leaving behind neutron stars or black holes. Carl Sagan's phrase — "we are made of star stuff" — is literally true: the calcium in our bones and the iron in our blood were manufactured in dead stars.

The expanding universe and the Big Bang

In 1929, Hubble discovered that galaxies are moving away from us, and the faster the farther they are (by measuring the redshift of their light — the cosmic Doppler effect). The conclusion: the universe is expanding. Rewinding that expansion leads to a hot, dense beginning about 13.8 billion years ago — the Big Bang. The decisive proof came in 1965 with the discovery of the cosmic microwave background, the thermal "echo" of that initial instant, now cooled to 2.7 K and detectable in every direction of the sky.

The great recent upheaval came from two puzzles. First, galaxies rotate too fast: the visible matter does not have enough gravity to hold them together — mass is missing. So dark matter is postulated, which emits no light and shows up only through gravity. Second, in 1998 it was discovered that the expansion of the universe is accelerating, pushed by something dubbed dark energy. Adding it all up, the composition of the universe, according to data from the Planck satellite (2018), is disconcerting:

ComponentFraction of the universeWhat it is
Dark energy~68%Unknown cause of the accelerating expansion; consistent with a "cosmological constant"
Dark matter~27%Invisible matter, detected only through gravity; unknown nature
Ordinary matter~5%Everything we know: stars, planets, gas, us — atoms

Table 2 — Composition of the universe (Planck 2018). About 95% of the cosmos is made of substances whose physical nature we do not know — perhaps the greatest open scandal in science.

In other words: everything physics has studied in four centuries — all the matter made of atoms — is only 5% of the universe. The other 95% is of unknown nature. It is worth stressing how honest the names are: "dark matter" and "dark energy" are labels for our ignorance, not explanations. Recent data from the DESI survey (2024–2025) even suggest that dark energy may not be constant, but vary over time — which, if confirmed, would force a revision of the standard cosmological model (see Active controversies).

Historical path ¶

Physics does not advance by linear accumulation, but through revolutions that reformulate the foundations — and through long periods of consolidation between them. Knowing that history is understanding why the theories have the form they do, and why each one delimits its domain of validity.

Scientific revolution1543–1687 — from Copernicus to Newton. Copernicus removes the Earth from the center; Galileo fuses mathematics and experiment and formulates inertia; Kepler extracts the laws of orbits from observation. In 1687, Newton's Principia unifies heaven and Earth under the same laws of motion and gravitation — physics is born as a mathematical science.

The Newtonian synthesis reigned for two centuries, so successful that by the end of the nineteenth century many judged physics nearly complete — only "two small problems" remained. In the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, it was refined by the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulations, and physics expanded into new domains: thermodynamics, driven by the Industrial Revolution and the study of steam engines (Carnot, Clausius, Kelvin), and electromagnetism, which Oersted, Faraday, and above all Maxwell (1865) gathered into a unified theory of electricity, magnetism, and light.

The double revolution1900–1927 — quantum theory and relativity. The "two small problems" (blackbody radiation and the null result of the Michelson-Morley experiment on the ether) blew up the foundations. Planck (1900) quantizes energy; Einstein rewrites space and time (1905) and gravity (1915); Bohr, Heisenberg, Schrödinger, Dirac, and Born build quantum mechanics (1925–1927). In 25 years, classical physics went from final theory to limiting case.

The postwar era was the age of particle physics and consolidation. Quantum field theory matured (the quantum electrodynamics of Feynman, Schwinger, and Tomonaga in the 1940s became the most precise theory ever tested), and between 1960 and 1975 the Standard Model brought together three of the four forces. In parallel, general relativity, dormant for decades, was reborn with Big Bang cosmology and the study of black holes.

Contemporary physics lives in a peculiar situation: it possesses two extraordinarily successful theories — the Standard Model (the very small) and general relativity (the very large) — which are, however, mutually incompatible in the regimes where both should hold (black holes, the Big Bang). Unifying them into a quantum gravity is the great open problem inherited from the twentieth century, still without a tested solution.

Physics in Brazil ¶

Brazilian physics is little more than a century old, but it has produced moments of world-class standing. Its founding landmark in research is the arrival of European physicists at the newly created University of São Paulo (USP, 1934), among them the Italian Gleb Wataghin, who trained the country's first generation of experimental and theoretical physicists.

Brazilian landmark1947 — the discovery of the pi meson (pion). The São Paulo native César Lattes, with Giuseppe Occhialini and Cecil Powell (Bristol), identified the pi meson in photographic plates exposed to cosmic radiation in the Andes — the particle Yukawa had predicted as the mediator of the strong nuclear force. The following year, Lattes took part in the first artificial production of the pion at the Berkeley cyclotron. Powell received the 1950 Nobel; Lattes, then 26, became the most celebrated Brazilian physicist.

The prestige of the discovery drove the institutionalization of the field. In 1949 the Brazilian Center for Physics Research (CBPF) was founded in Rio, and in 1951 the CNPq, the agency that would structure the funding of national science — Lattes was central to both. Strong groups later formed in theoretical areas, notably around Mário Schenberg (astrophysics; the "Urca process" of supernova cooling, named after a casino in Rio) and José Leite Lopes (particle physics).

Today the largest bet on infrastructure is Sirius, a fourth-generation synchrotron light source operated by LNLS/CNPEM in Campinas — the largest and most complex scientific facility ever built in the country. A synchrotron accelerates electrons to nearly the speed of light to generate extremely intense radiation, used to "see" the structure of matter at the atomic scale (materials, proteins, drugs). In 2025, of the 14 planned experimental stations, 10 are already operating, serving hundreds of research projects a year. Alongside it, Brazil takes part in large international collaborations, from the LHC (CERN) to cosmic-ray observatories such as the Pierre Auger Observatory in Argentina, with a strong Brazilian presence.

Central questions ¶

These are the questions that organize fundamental physics today — genuinely open, in the sense that competent physicists disagree about them or simply do not know the answer.

  1. How can general relativity and quantum mechanics be united into a single theory of quantum gravity, able to describe black holes and the Big Bang without infinite singularities?
  2. What, physically, are dark matter and dark energy, which account for ~95% of the universe? Are they new particles and fields, or signs that our theory of gravity is wrong?
  3. Why is the universe made of matter and almost no antimatter, if the Big Bang should have produced them in equal amounts?
  4. What does the quantum wave function represent — a real state of the world, or just our knowledge about it? (the "measurement problem")
  5. Is there physics beyond the Standard Model? Why is gravity so absurdly weaker than the other forces (the hierarchy problem)?
  6. How does macroscopic irreversibility (the arrow of time) emerge from reversible microscopic laws, and why did the universe begin with such low entropy?
  7. Is there an explanation for high-temperature superconductivity, and is a room-temperature superconductor possible?
  8. Could the fundamental constants of nature (the electron's mass, the strength of the interactions) have had other values — and why do they have exactly those that allow stars and life to exist (the "fine-tuning" problem)?

Tools and methods ¶

Physics produces and validates knowledge through a cycle between theory and experiment, bound by a non-negotiable requirement: every claim must, in principle, be testable by measurement. The methods below are the gears of that cycle.

Mathematical modeling: translating a phenomenon into equations that capture its essential variables and ignore the rest. The power of physics comes from the fact that these equations make quantitative predictions — not "the object falls," but "it falls a distance d = ½gt²". The precision of the prediction is what gets put to the test.

Controlled experiment: isolating one variable, varying it, and measuring the effect while keeping the others fixed. It was Galileo's innovation — inclined planes to "dilute" gravity and measure the fall — and it remains at the heart of the method, from the benchtop lab to the great colliders.

Precision measurement: much of frontier physics is the art of measuring ever better. It was a discrepancy in the sixth decimal place that revealed antimatter, and another that pinned down the magnetic moment of the electron — the most precisely confirmed quantity in science, with theory-experiment agreement to more than ten decimal places.

Large instruments: modern physics depends on colossal machines — particle accelerators (LHC), space telescopes (James Webb, Planck), gravitational-wave detectors (LIGO), and synchrotron light sources (Sirius). They cost billions and bring together thousands of scientists, marking the passage of physics from the lone genius to collaborative "big science."

Computational simulation: when the equations have no analytic solution (most real systems), one simulates numerically. It has become a "third pillar," between theory and experiment, essential from weather forecasting to lattice quantum chromodynamics (which computes the mass of the proton from the quarks).

The demarcation criterion: what separates physics from speculations that imitate it? Karl Popper's classic answer is falsifiability — a scientific theory must forbid some possible observation, exposing itself to refutation. Theories that "explain everything" and risk nothing are not physics. That criterion, today refined and debated, lies behind the wariness toward proposals such as certain versions of string theory, hard to test experimentally (see Active controversies).

Connections with other fields ¶

Mathematics: it is the native language of physics, but the relationship goes further than use. Whole areas of mathematics (calculus, with Newton and Leibniz; differential geometry, which general relativity demanded) were born or flourished out of physics's demand, and physical problems still generate new mathematics today. The "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics in the natural sciences" (Wigner) is itself a philosophical puzzle.

Chemistry: it is, to a large extent, applied physics — the chemical bond is electromagnetism plus quantum mechanics, and the periodic table follows from the quantum structure of atoms. The border between physical chemistry and chemical physics is today almost invisible.

Biology: biophysics applies physical methods to living systems — from the X-ray diffraction that revealed the double helix of DNA to the mechanics of proteins and the thermodynamics of metabolism. Sirius, in Brazil, is used precisely for this.

Philosophy: physics feeds and is fed by the philosophy of science (what a theory is, what an explanation is, the demarcation criterion) and by metaphysics (the nature of time, causality, determinism). The interpretations of quantum mechanics are as much physics as philosophy. See the Epistemology compendium.

Computer science: quantum computing translates principles of physics (superposition, entanglement) into computing power; and physics itself uses machine learning to sift through data from colliders and telescopes. There is also the deep connection between information and entropy, which links thermodynamics and information theory. See the Artificial Intelligence compendium.

Engineering and technology: the route from basic physics to application defines the modern world — the transistor (condensed matter) made the digital age, the laser (quantum optics) fiber-optic communication, and magnetic resonance (nuclear physics and superconductivity) medical imaging.

Active controversies ¶

What does the wave function represent? (interpretations of quantum mechanics)

Copenhagen / epistemic

The wave function represents our knowledge or information about the system, not a physical reality. Measurement "collapses" it because it updates what we know. There is nothing more to explain; asking what "really" happens before measurement is going beyond what the theory licenses.

Many-worlds / ontic

The wave function is real and never collapses: all possible outcomes occur, each in a "branch" of the universe that splits at every measurement. It preserves determinism and Schrödinger evolution, at the cost of an unimaginable number of parallel universes.

There is also the Bohm interpretation (particles with real trajectories guided by a pilot wave, deterministic but non-local), objective collapse (GRW, which modifies the equation), and QBism (the wave function as the observer's Bayesian bet). None makes an experimental prediction distinct from the others — for now, the dispute is over what the same mathematics means.

How to go beyond the Standard Model? (the road to quantum gravity)

String theory

Particles would be vibrations of one-dimensional "strings," in a spacetime of 10 or 11 dimensions. It unifies all the forces, including gravity, with mathematical elegance — but to this day has made no prediction testable at accessible energies, which leads critics to ask whether it is science or mathematics.

Loop quantum gravity

Quantizes spacetime directly, which would be granular at the Planck scale, without requiring extra dimensions or new particles. It preserves general relativity, but has difficulty recovering smooth spacetime and the matter of the Standard Model.

Is dark energy constant or does it evolve?

Cosmological constant (ΛCDM)

Dark energy is a constant — the energy of the vacuum itself — uniform in space and unchanging in time. It is the standard model of cosmology, fitting most data with few parameters. Simple and successful, though it does not explain why the constant has the value it does.

Dynamical dark energy

Recent data from the DESI survey (2024–2025) suggest that dark energy may vary over cosmic time (quintessence), weakening. If confirmed by more data, it would overturn the cosmological constant and open a new chapter — but the statistical significance is still debated.

Dark matter: new particle or modified gravity?

Particle (dark matter)

There exists an as-yet-undetected particle, outside the Standard Model, that makes up dark matter. It explains galaxy formation, gravitational lensing, and the background radiation well — but decades of direct searches for that particle have not found it.

Modified gravity (MOND)

Nothing is missing; our law of gravity fails at low accelerations. It fits the rotation curves of galaxies without a new particle, but struggles at larger scales (clusters, background radiation), where dark matter does better.

Open questions ¶

Beyond the controversies with defined sides, there are frontiers where physics does not yet have even consensual candidate answers.

Quantum gravity

There is no tested theory uniting general relativity and quantum mechanics. Inside a black hole and at the first instant of the Big Bang, the two contradict each other — and no current experiment reaches the energy scale (Planck) where the answer would be decided.

The measurement problem

The Schrödinger equation describes superpositions that evolve smoothly; measurement always delivers a single, definite outcome. What exactly counts as a "measurement," and why it breaks the superposition, remains without consensual answer — the heart of the rival interpretations.

The fine-tuning of the constants

Small changes in the fundamental constants would make stars, atoms, or chemistry impossible. Is this coincidence, a necessity not yet understood, or a sign of a "multiverse" where all values occur and we inhabit the one that permits observers? There is no way to decide experimentally today.

The arrow of time

Why does time have a direction, if almost all the fundamental laws do not distinguish one? The usual answer — the low initial entropy of the universe — merely pushes the question back to: why did the universe begin so ordered?

Where to start ¶

1
Overview without heavy formulas: Six Easy Pieces (Richard Feynman) is the best entry point — six chapters from the legendary Feynman Lectures chosen for requiring little math, with the physical intuition of one of the greatest teachers of the twentieth century. They cover atoms, energy, gravitation, and quantum behavior.
2
The base with calculus: a serious course begins with Halliday, Resnick & Walker — Fundamentals of Physics, the canonical introductory textbook. It requires basic calculus and covers mechanics, thermodynamics, electromagnetism, and waves with thousands of problems — the backbone of any undergraduate degree.
3
Modern physics for the general public: for relativity and cosmology without equations, A Brief History of Time (Stephen Hawking); for quantum strangeness, the introductory material is vast, but start with the quantum section of the Feynman Lectures before any popularization — it avoids the common distortions.
4
Going deeper into one subfield (undergraduate level): pick a pillar and go to the dedicated text — Griffiths for electromagnetism and for the introduction to quantum mechanics (didactic and rigorous), Kittel & Kroemer for thermal physics. This is where intuition becomes computational skill.
5
Frontier and maximum rigor: for those who master the core, the Course of Theoretical Physics by Landau & Lifshitz is the advanced reference; and following current research is done through arXiv.org (free preprints) and Physical Review (APS), the gold standard of peer review.

Synthesis ¶

The guiding question — why does nature let itself be written in mathematics, and how far does that alphabet reach? — runs through everything we have seen. As for the first part, physics does not answer why, but demonstrates that it does: from F = m·a to Maxwell's equations, from E = mc2 to the Schrödinger equation, a handful of compact relations describe phenomena separated by dozens of orders of magnitude. The "unreasonable effectiveness of mathematics" remains a puzzle — but the historical pattern is clear: every great advance was a unification, showing that phenomena taken to be distinct were faces of a single law (heaven and Earth in Newton; electricity, magnetism, and light in Maxwell; space, time, and gravity in Einstein; three forces in the Standard Model).

As for the second part — how far it reaches — the answer is humble. Each theory has its domain, and at the borders between them present-day physics breaks: general relativity and quantum mechanics contradict each other where both should hold, and 95% of the content of the universe escapes any description — dark matter and dark energy are names for our ignorance. The most successful theory in history (the Standard Model) is, at the same time, the clearest proof that there is physics left to discover.

From this emerges an implication the body of the compendium did not state directly: the success of physics generates its own limits. It was the precision of Newton that exposed, two centuries later, the anomalies only relativity and quantum theory would explain; it is the precision of the Standard Model and general relativity that today makes visible what they do not explain. The history of physics does not converge toward a calm "final theory" — each well-measured answer opens a deeper question. Perhaps that, more than any equation, is the true method of physics: turning vague ignorance into precise ignorance — and it is the precision of the question that, in the end, becomes knowledge.
